# Discussion

MetaboLiteLearner 2.0 should be read as a balanced methods paper. The central contribution is not a claim that the current representation is final, but a demonstration that fragmentation-first learning remains viable after the front end is changed from fixed peak windows to learned components. On the present benchmark, the method reduces the assay to 12 learned components, 12 fold-change peaks, and four latent components while preserving a predictive and interpretable response space. That is enough to establish that joint extraction is a meaningful advance over treating preprocessing as a fixed external step.

The positive result is the persistence of interpretable structure after this change in representation. The 2.0 workflow still produces component spectra, effect estimates, and latent directions that can be discussed in biochemical terms without requiring every analyte to be identified in advance. In practice, that keeps the method useful as a prioritization layer. The model can rank where the most informative rewiring signal appears, separate shared from lineage-specific behavior, and provide a compact latent summary that can be inspected rather than only optimized.

The principal current limitation is oversplitting of dominant retention-time neighborhoods. Several nearby components are being learned where a more stable representation would likely merge them. This matters because parsimony is not an aesthetic preference here; it affects how easily the learned representation can be read as chemistry rather than as decomposition artifact. The next algorithmic step should therefore be directed squarely at this problem: stronger diversity regularization, explicit component merging, or both. Future versions should be judged less by whether they extract more components and more by whether they extract fewer redundant ones without sacrificing the observed fold-change signal.

The current result also sharpens the boundary of inference. Post hoc Fiehn and KEGG matches are useful for contextualization, but they do not convert learned components into definitive metabolite identities. The fragmentation-space learner still operates at the level of reproducible component geometry, not complete chemical resolution. Likewise, the small study size and the downstream mixed-effects stage impose practical limits on the strength of inferential claims. The present paper therefore supports a constrained conclusion: the workflow is useful for representation, prioritization, and structured comparison across cell states, but it is not yet a substitute for targeted validation or mechanistic follow-up.

That constraint does not weaken the value of the 2.0 rewrite. It makes the contribution more concrete. The 2024 paper established that fragmentation spectra could support prediction before full annotation [10]. The present paper shows how that idea behaves when the intermediate representation is itself learned from the GC/MS matrices. In that sense, MetaboLiteLearner 2.0 is already a useful result: it provides a working component-first method, a readable latent space, and a clear next target for improving the representation without abandoning interpretability.
