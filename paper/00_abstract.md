# Abstract

MetaboLiteLearner 2.0 replaces fixed-window peak extraction with a component-first workflow that jointly models chromatographic structure, fragmentation structure, and sample-level abundance variation in GC/MS data. The goal is to test whether metabolic rewiring can still be learned from fragmentation structure when the intermediate representation is itself learned rather than imposed by heuristic peak windows.

The method was evaluated on the MDA-MB-231 parental, brain-homing, lung-homing, and media benchmark system used in the original MetaboLiteLearner work [10]. Starting from aligned scan-mode GC/MS matrices, the 2.0 pipeline learned 12 joint components, produced a 12-row fold-change table, and selected a four-component partial least squares model as the compact predictive summary. The selected model achieved a fitted correlation of `r = 0.92` and a leave-one-out correlation of `r = 0.67`, while the four latent components captured approximately 84% of response variance.

Post hoc reference matching associated the learned components primarily with carbohydrates and organic acids, indicating that the new representation retains chemically interpretable structure without requiring metabolite identification at the front of the pipeline. The main current limitation is oversplitting of dominant retention-time regions into several nearby components, which reduces parsimony even when predictive structure is preserved.

These results show that fragmentation-space learning remains viable after replacing peak-window summarization with a learned joint representation. MetaboLiteLearner 2.0 therefore advances the original fragmentation-first idea from a proof of feasibility to an executable component-learning workflow, while also making the main remaining representation problem explicit.
