# Introduction

Cells continually rewire metabolism in response to nutrient availability, tissue context, and stress, yet metabolomics still struggles to move cleanly from signal capture to biological interpretation [1]. Targeted workflows are powerful when the analyte set is known in advance, but they miss unexpected chemistry. Untargeted workflows broaden coverage, but they often stop at detection and annotation, leaving the relationship between spectral evidence and biological change only partially resolved [2, 3]. A useful method should therefore do more than enumerate compounds. It should organize the metabolome as a structured response to cellular state while preserving a path back to chemistry.

The original MetaboLiteLearner paper introduced one answer to that problem by treating fragmentation spectra themselves as predictive objects [10]. Rather than requiring full metabolite identification before modeling, the 2024 study asked whether scan-mode electron ionization spectra already contain enough structure to predict how metabolites change across related cell states. That study established feasibility. It showed that fragmentation-first learning could recover biologically meaningful rewiring signal in a metastatic breast-cancer benchmark and that the resulting latent space was interpretable enough to discuss shared and lineage-specific metabolic behavior.

MetaboLiteLearner 2.0 addresses the next bottleneck in that idea: the representation used before learning. In the earlier workflow, the model operated on spectra created by a peak-window summarization step. That approach is serviceable, but it commits to fixed chromatographic boundaries before the learner ever sees the data. Co-eluting structure, redundant windows, and uncertainty in peak boundaries are therefore handled by preprocessing rather than by the model itself. For a fragmentation-first method, that is a real limitation because the quality of the learned response map is capped by the quality of the peak list it inherits.

The central contribution of this paper is to replace that fixed-window front end with a learned component-first representation. MetaboLiteLearner 2.0 starts from aligned GC/MS matrices and learns joint components that each carry chromatographic structure, spectral structure, and sample-level abundance variation. Those learned abundances are then used for fold-change estimation and for the downstream partial least squares learner. The resulting pipeline remains interpretable, but it makes the intermediate representation part of the model rather than a separate heuristic preprocessing choice.

The current paper reports that 2.0 workflow on the MDA-MB-231 parental, brain-homing, lung-homing, and media benchmark. The goal is not to claim a final solution to representation learning in metabolomics. It is to show what the current component-first workflow already does well, where it improves on the earlier peak-centric pipeline, and where it still fails. The main present limitation is oversplitting of dominant retention-time regions into multiple nearby components. That limitation matters because it makes the model more fragmented than the underlying chemistry likely is. It also makes the next engineering target clear: preserve the predictive signal while learning a more parsimonious and stable component geometry.
